Why Price Really Moves

Before learning the entry model, you need to understand WHO moves NQ and WHY. This changes everything about how you read a chart — especially during the Asia session.

🏦

Smart Money

Banks, hedge funds, and institutions move hundreds of millions of dollars. They can't just click "buy" — they need massive liquidity to fill orders without moving price against themselves. They need your stop losses.

THE INSTITUTIONS
👥

Retail Traders

Regular traders using chart patterns, support/resistance, and indicators. They cluster stop losses in predictable places — just above recent highs or just below recent lows. This makes them easy to hunt.

THE CROWD
🎯

Liquidity Pools

When many traders put stop losses above a high or below a low, those stops form a pool of orders. Smart money drives price to those stops, fills their own position in the opposite direction, then moves price the other way.

THE TARGET
🔄

The Manipulation Cycle

Drive up → grab sell stops above highs → short the market → drive down. Or drive down → grab buy stops below lows → go long → drive up. This cycle repeats at every timeframe — including Asia session.

The 4-Step Entry Model

Every trade — no exceptions — requires all 4 steps in order. This is the same framework running inside the NQ War Room bot. Miss one step and you do not have a trade.

THE COMPLETE 4-STEP TRADE — BEARISH EXAMPLE (SHORT)

1

HTF PD Array — Where Are We?

On the 4-hour or daily chart, identify which zone price is sitting in. For this playbook, a valid HTF zone must be one of three types: a BPR (Balanced Price Range), an iFVG (an FVG that was previously traded through and flipped polarity), or a Layered OB+FVG (an Order Block and a Fair Value Gap formed in different sessions, spatially overlapping). Standard Order Blocks alone do not qualify.

Premium zone: only look for SHORT trades — price is expensive
Discount zone: only look for LONG trades — price is cheap
Equilibrium (50%): wait — no edge until price reaches a zone
No valid HTF zone present = No trade, full stop
2

Check Point Sweep + FVG — The Hunt

Once price is inside the HTF zone, watch for it to sweep a Check Point (an internal liquidity pool — equal highs/lows, prior swing, resting stops) with a wick on the 1m or 5m timeframe. The wick must reject — it closes back inside the zone, not beyond it. The same sweeping leg must create a Fair Value Gap as it reverses out of the level.

Bullish sweep: wick below an internal low, candle closes back above → SSL hunted
Bearish sweep: wick above an internal high, candle closes back below → BSL hunted
Confirm on: 1m or 5m chart wick rejection — close must be back inside the zone
No FVG from the sweeping leg = setup not armed yet, wait
3

Displacement + MSS Confirmation

After the sweep, a single strong candle with a clear body (not a wick) must close through the Fair Value Gap in the opposite direction. This is the displacement candle — it inverts the FVG into an iFVG. Additionally, the 5m chart must show a Market Structure Shift: the last swing point formed during the sweep wick is broken. Both must be true. The volume on the displacement candle must be ≥ 1.5× the 20-period volume average on the 1m — this is the Volume Filter. Low-volume displacement = the algorithm isn't running with conviction.

Long MSS: displacement closes UP through FVG + breaks swing high from sweep wick
Short MSS: displacement closes DOWN through FVG + breaks swing low from sweep wick
Volume filter: displacement candle volume ≥ 1.5× SMA(volume, 20) on 1m
Wick displacement (not body) = does not count — wait for a body close
Below-average volume displacement = SKIP — setup fails the volume filter
4

iFVG Entry — The Execution

After the MSS and displacement candle, price will pull back into the iFVG zone. This is your entry zone. Place a limit order at the near edge of the iFVG before price arrives. Stop loss goes at the last swing low (for longs) or last swing high (for shorts) — the actual wick of the sweep. Target is 30 points (1R) or 60 points (2R) when the next HTF liquidity pool sits beyond 60 points. Enter on the first retest — if price runs without pulling back, you missed it. Do not chase.

Entry: limit order at near edge of iFVG on first pullback — set before price arrives
Stop loss: the sweep wick low/high — this level defines your risk
Target: 30 pts (1R) default or 60 pts (2R) when structure supports it
Limit order cancels: 15 minutes after displacement candle OR at 11:00 PM ET
Missed the first retest = trade is over. Next setup is always coming.
Section 03

The iFVG — Your Entry Zone

📐

What is an FVG?

Take three consecutive candles. If candle 1's low is higher than candle 3's high (bullish) or candle 1's high is lower than candle 3's low (bearish) — price moved so fast it left an imbalance. This gap is a magnet that price eventually fills.

FVG BASICS
🔄

What Makes it an iFVG?

When a reversal candle closes THROUGH the original FVG with a strong body in the opposite direction, it "inverts" the gap. The zone that was a bearish imbalance becomes bullish support. This is the signal the bot fires on.

iFVG SIGNAL
🏗️

Layered OB + FVG

An Order Block from one session (e.g. Monday) and an FVG from a later session (e.g. Thursday) that spatially overlap. The OB and FVG can have opposite polarity — what matters is the dense concentration of resting orders at that price level.

HIGH-CONVICTION ZONE
⏱️

Enter on the First Touch

When price pulls back into the iFVG zone after the MSS, that first touch is the entry. Set your limit order at the near edge of the iFVG before price arrives. Miss the first touch and the trade is over. Never chase.

Section 06

Key Terms — Plain English

Learn these terms before you start looking at charts. Every term connects directly to the 4-step model.

BPR — Balanced Price Range

A stacked Fair Value Gap where both sides of an inefficiency are present — a bullish FVG and a bearish FVG overlap at the same price level, creating a balanced zone. This is one of the three valid HTF zones for this playbook. Where both sides of the market have unfilled orders.

Valid HTF Zone Type 1
iFVG — Inverted Fair Value Gap

A Fair Value Gap (4H or higher) that price has traded through in the opposite direction. The zone flipped polarity — old bearish imbalance becomes bullish support, or vice versa. This flip is what creates the highest-conviction entry zones in the playbook.

Valid HTF Zone Type 2
Layered OB + FVG

An Order Block and a Fair Value Gap that were formed in different sessions (temporally separated) but occupy the same or adjacent price space. The OB and FVG do not need to share polarity — what matters is the dense concentration of resting orders at that level. The FVG typically sits between current price and the OB, meaning price crosses through the FVG to reach the OB.

Valid HTF Zone Type 3
Check Point

An internal liquidity pool sitting INSIDE a larger HTF zone. Equal highs, equal lows, or prior swing points that resting stops have clustered around. When price sweeps a check point inside the zone (not just any random level), that sweep is what arms the setup. This is more selective than a standard liquidity sweep.

Step 2 Trigger
Displacement

A fast, one-directional move with a strong candle body (not a wick) that closes through the FVG created by the sweep leg. One displacement candle is enough. This candle must show volume ≥ 1.5× the 20-period average — that volume confirms the algorithm is running with real conviction, not a false move.

Step 3 — Volume Filter
Market Structure Shift (MSS)

When the last swing high/low formed DURING the sweep wick gets broken, structure has officially shifted. On this playbook, MSS is confirmed on the 5m timeframe. Without MSS, the displacement alone is not enough — you need the structural break to confirm the reversal is real.

Step 3 — Confirmation
Premium vs Discount

The 50% midpoint of the current swing range. Above 50% = Premium (expensive) = short setups only. Below 50% = Discount (cheap) = long setups only. Used as a filter alongside the 100 EMA to confirm HTF zone alignment. Never take a long in premium or a short in discount.

Bias Filter
Same-Color Candle Bias Rule

On Daily: last 2 candles same color = bias confirmed. On 4H: last 2 candles same color + matching Daily = confirmed. On 1H: last 4 candles same color + matching D/4H = confirmed. If any timeframe breaks the alignment or has mixed colors, there is no clean bias = no trade tonight. This is the primary bias engine for this playbook.

Section 2.1 — Bias Check
Second-Chance Rule

If the initial stop loss is taken out by a sweep of the stop level itself, that stop-out can qualify as a new check point. If the FULL setup sequence (steps 2–4) re-forms off this new sweep, ONE more entry is allowed. This is the only re-entry condition in the playbook. The second-chance trade must not push the session past the daily loss limit.

Risk Rule
HH / HL / LH / LL

Higher Highs + Higher Lows = uptrend (bullish, look for longs only). Lower Highs + Lower Lows = downtrend (bearish, look for shorts only). Neither present = consolidation = close the laptop. This structural check is the final disqualifier before the window opens.

Section 2.5 — Structure Check
BSL / SSL — Buyside / Sellside Liquidity

BSL: stop losses from shorts sitting ABOVE highs. Smart money drives price up to grab these, fills their own shorts, then drives down. SSL: stop losses from longs sitting BELOW lows. Equal highs = massive BSL cluster. Equal lows = massive SSL cluster. These are the check points you're hunting inside the HTF zones.

Liquidity Mechanics
Volume Filter (D10)

A hard disqualifier: the displacement candle (Step 3) must trade on volume ≥ 1.5× the 20-period simple moving average of volume on the 1m timeframe. If displacement is on below-average volume, the algorithm isn't running with conviction. The iFVG may look valid but the move won't carry to target. Hermes checks this automatically.

Disqualifier D10 — Hard Filter
Section 07

Mistakes That Destroy Accounts

Knowing the setup is 50% of it. These mistakes will erase profits even when your analysis is right.

✗

Trading Without a Valid HTF Zone

The 5m setup looks perfect — sweep, FVG, displacement, MSS, all four steps. But price is not inside a BPR, iFVG, or Layered OB+FVG on the 4H or higher. You enter anyway. This is the most common reason clean-looking setups fail. Without HTF context, the "algorithm" you're trading is just noise. The bot checks this first, before scoring anything else.

✓ No valid 4H+ zone present = no score from Hermes = no trade. Playbook Section 1.5.
✗

Taking a Setup Before 8:30 PM

The window opens at 8:15 but the first 15 minutes is setup formation, not execution. Price is establishing direction, finding its range, generating the levels that become the evening's sweep targets. Setups that trigger in this window have a significantly lower completion rate. The urgency to be "in early" is a trap.

✓ Sweet spot starts at 8:30 PM. Setups before 8:30 = treat with extreme skepticism.
✗

Ignoring the Volume Filter

Displacement looks strong. Body close through the FVG. MSS on 5m confirmed. But the volume on the displacement candle is 0.8× average, not 1.5×. You enter anyway. Price retraces back through the iFVG, your limit fills, and the move never carries. Low-volume displacement means institutions are not behind this move — it's a retail-driven fake.

✓ Displacement volume must be ≥ 1.5× SMA(20) on 1m. Below threshold = Hermes flags D10 = skip.
✗

Trading Consolidation

The chart looks "active" but price is actually ranging — overlapping wicks, no clear swing structure, candles that partially retrace each other. You see what looks like a sweep and enter. The market chops through your stop and then through your would-be target. Consolidation kills this playbook because it depends on directional algorithmic delivery.

✓ If you cannot identify HH/HL or LH/LL in 30 seconds on 1H + 4H — close the laptop. Not debatable.
✗

Misaligned HTF Bias

Daily candles are green (bullish bias). But 4H candles are mixed or red. You go long anyway based on the daily alone. The 4H structure is telling you something the daily isn't — a local pullback that could be the start of a real correction. All three timeframes (D/4H/1H) must show same-color alignment or no trade exists.

✓ All three timeframes must agree. One timeframe against the bias = no trade tonight.
✗

Abusing the Second-Chance Rule

You took a loss. You invoke the second-chance rule. But the stop-out didn't form a clean new check point — it was just random slippage. You force a re-entry. This is not a second chance, it's revenge trading with extra steps. The second-chance rule has a hard condition: the FULL 4-step sequence must re-form cleanly off the stop-out level.

✓ Second-chance requires all 4 steps from scratch. If you're rationalizing, you're not second-chancing.
✗

Trading After Rule A or Rule B Triggers

You've taken 2 losses (Rule A trigger). The session is over. But a "perfect" setup forms at 9:45 PM. You enter. You lose again. Now you're at 3 losses, the Rule D target math is broken, and you've burned through $540 on a session where you should have stopped at $360. Rules A and B exist because 2 losses in a session is a signal — something is off in the market or in your head.

✓ Rule A = 2 losses = session over. Rule B = $360 hit = session over. No exceptions. No "just one more."
✗

Giving Back After Rule D Triggers

You hit your 30-point target. First trade of the session, +$180, beautiful. A new setup immediately forms. You take it "since the session is still open." You lose. Now you've given back your winner and are down $180 on a session that was up $180. Rule D exists for one reason: the highest-probability trade of giving back a winner is the trade you take right after a winner.

✓ Target hit = session over. Laptop closes. This rule is non-negotiable.

Section 09

The Mental Game

The method works. What breaks traders is not the strategy — it's the space between knowing what to do and actually doing it when real money is on the line.

📊

Think in Probabilities

Even a 60% win rate means losing 4 out of 10 trades. A losing streak of 5–6 trades in a row can happen while doing everything right. Never change the model because of a streak. The bot keeps trading the model regardless. So should you — until the data tells you otherwise.

🧱

Build Data First

Run the QuantConnect backtest before trading live. Spend 30 sessions in observe-only mode with Hermes logging setups you didn't take. Build the Supabase dataset. Confidence comes from evidence, not hope. The bot was built the same way: test first, trade second.

⏳

Most Sessions = No Trade

Forcing a trade when the setup isn't there is how accounts blow up. The best traders sit on their hands for an entire 2-hour session. No setup = no trade is a successful session. Hermes not firing an alert is not a failure — it's the playbook working correctly.

📓

Journal Every Session

At 11:00 PM, Hermes posts the session recap automatically. You write three lines: what was the bias, what was your execution grade (A/B/C/F), and one sentence on the biggest lesson. After 50 sessions, patterns in your wins and losses will emerge that no one can teach you.

🔬

3 Losses = Study, Not Trade

After 3 consecutive losing sessions, trading is paused. Not rested for a day and back at it — paused until a structured review finds the root cause. Was it the playbook? Your execution? The market regime? Answer that question before returning. Return to trading with a theory, not hope.

📈

Size Up on Data, Not Emotion

We start at 3 MNQ. We move to 4 MNQ after $500 banked. We move to 5 MNQ after $1,500 banked. Any session that erases the threshold steps back down. You do not size up because you "feel good" or had a 3-win streak. You size up because the data says you've earned it.

"The goal is not to trade more. The goal is to wait for the setup where all 4 steps are present, inside a valid HTF zone, with volume confirmation — and then execute without hesitation. Every great trade starts with the discipline to wait. Every blown account starts with a trade that 'looked good enough.'"
